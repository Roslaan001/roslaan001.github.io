There's a particular kind of paralysis that hits smart, capable people. They want to get into something new — a field, a skill, a practice — but they don't want anyone to see them being bad at it first.

So they wait. They consume. They prepare in private. And often, they never start.

I've been there. And I've noticed that the thing that got me unstuck — every time — was the discomfort of doing something visibly, imperfectly, in front of others.

## The Expert Trap

Here's the thing about expertise: it makes beginners feel more embarrassed about being beginners. The more you know, the more acutely you feel the distance between where you are and where the people you admire are.

But this is an illusion built on a very skewed sample. You see the polished outputs — the finished essays, the slick products, the confident talks — not the years of fumbling that preceded them.

Everyone you respect was once bad at the thing they're now good at. Everyone. The difference is they kept going anyway, usually in some public-enough way that created accountability and feedback.

## What "Learning in Public" Actually Means

It doesn't mean broadcasting every half-formed thought or publishing work you're not proud of. It means removing the perfectionism filter that keeps you silent until you feel ready — when "ready" is a moving target that never quite arrives.

It might look like:
- Writing a blog post about something you just learned (meta, I know)
- Sharing an early project even though it's obviously rough
- Asking a question in a community even though you're afraid it sounds stupid
- Showing someone your work before you think it's done

> The gap between where you are and where you want to be only closes through repetition and feedback. Neither of those happens in isolation.

## The Feedback Loop You're Missing

Private practice has a fundamental flaw: you can't get feedback on what no one sees. And without feedback, you can't calibrate. You might be practicing the wrong things confidently. You might be a lot better than you think and have no idea. You might be stuck on a problem that someone else could unstick in five minutes.

Learning in public collapses that feedback loop. It's uncomfortable because you're exposed. But exposure is, almost always, the point.

## The Permission You're Waiting For

If you've been waiting for permission to share the thing you're working on, to write about the thing you're learning, to ask the question you're afraid sounds dumb — this is it.

You're allowed to be early in the process. You're allowed to not have it figured out. The people worth learning from respect earnest beginners. The ones who don't weren't going to help you anyway.

Start where you are. Share what you have. Learn from what comes back.

The discomfort is the whole point.
